Dr. Raymond Okafor — GP, 54
Raymond was born in Peckham to Nigerian parents who had crossed an ocean on the strength of a belief in education so total it was almost religious. His father drove buses; his mother cleaned offices in the City at hours that meant Raymond often woke to an empty flat and got himself and his sister to school. He was clever and serious and he understood early that medicine was the door his parents had bought him with their backs. He went through it without ever quite asking whether he wanted to — the question seemed almost obscene, given what it had cost to be allowed to ask it. He qualified, did his rotations, and chose general practice because it offered something the hospital didn't: the long view, the same faces over years, families he could follow like rivers.
He is fifty-four now and a partner at a practice in a Midlands town — Leicester's outer edge, a surgery in a converted Victorian villa with a car park that floods. He has been there twenty-two years. He has delivered the news of pregnancies and the news of cancers to the same people. There is a tenderness in him still, but it has gone the way of a muscle held too long in one position: present, capable, but no longer quite reaching its full length.
The thing about Raymond's days is that they have narrowed to a groove so smooth he no longer feels the sides. He rises at 6:30, the same Radio 4 voices, the same two slices of toast standing at the counter, the same drive — Saffron Lane, the Aylestone roundabout, the back way past the rec — a route his hands could drive without him. Surgery from eight. Ten-minute appointments, sometimes eight minutes when they're slammed, and he is slammed most days. He sits. This is the part he doesn't examine: he sits for nine hours. Patient in the chair beside the desk, Raymond half-turned to the screen, the screen the real interlocutor, the human a sort of footnote to the record he's updating. He has become very good and very fast and somewhere in the speed the texture leaked out.
His body has gone quiet on him. He carries it around now rather than living in it — a soft heaviness at the middle that his own profession would flag in a patient, a stiffness in the knees on the stairs, a tiredness that sleep doesn't touch. He used to play five-a-side on Wednesdays with some of the registrars; he stopped maybe six years ago, a fixture quietly let lapse, and he could not now say when the last game was. He eats the canteen sandwich at his desk between patients. By evening there is a flatness in his senses that he reads as fatigue but which is closer to absence — the food has no real taste, the wine is just the thing that loosens the day's grip. He showers and the water is just water.
His wife, Comfort, is a paediatric nurse; they met in the doctors' mess in 1996 over terrible coffee and a shared exhaustion that felt, then, like aliveness. They have three children — the eldest doing law, the way these things repeat, the middle one travelling and worrying them, the youngest still at home doing A-levels and treating the house as a hotel with strong opinions. The marriage is warm. It is also, on weeknights, almost entirely silent in the contented way of two people too tired to perform. Saturdays are the shop, the garden centre sometimes, church on Sunday where his mother, now eighty-one and ferocious, still expects him in the third pew. Nothing new enters. Years ago they talked about Lagos, about taking the children to see where it all came from; the conversation comes up perhaps once a year and dissolves into the impossibility of locum cover.
What sits on Raymond's shelf — literally, in the spare room — is a guitar. A red Yamaha he bought himself at forty as a kind of promise, taught himself three Marley songs on, and has not lifted since the youngest was small. He passes the spare room. He thinks, occasionally, I should. The string he broke in 2014 is still curled in the case.
He noticed something last week. A patient, an old man, dying slowly and unbothered by it, said the days are very full, doctor, when you actually look at them — and Raymond drove the Aylestone roundabout afterward with a strange pressure behind his eyes, a sense of having been looking at his own days without seeing them. Then the next patient, and the next. The pressure passed.
A healer who has tended everyone's body but his own, until his own has gone dim and quiet beneath him like a fire banked and forgotten.

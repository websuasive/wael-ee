Alan Reddish — Finance Director, 57
Alan grew up in Stockport, lower-middle, a father in insurance and a mother who counted everything — pennies, calories, the days till payday — out of a wartime-inherited dread of not-enough. Alan inherited the counting without the dread, or rather he sublimated the dread into competence. He was good with numbers the way some people are good with faces; they arranged themselves for him. He did accountancy, qualified, moved through industry, and is now finance director of a mid-sized distribution company in Cheshire, a man with a strong handshake and a habit of glancing at his phone that he believes is subtler than it is.
The defining fact of Alan is that he is almost never in the room he is standing in. His attention runs perpetually one beat ahead — to the next meeting, the next quarter, the cash-flow forecast that lives behind his eyes like a screensaver that never fully sleeps. You can be talking to Alan and watch a part of him leave, a faint glaze, the mm, yes arriving a half-second late. He is not rude. He is elsewhere. He has been elsewhere for so long that here has become a place he visits rather than inhabits.
He is also, like the solicitor though he'd be surprised to be paired with him, claimed entirely by his commitments — but where Geoffrey's claims are other people's needs, Alan's are the self-set treadmill of a man who measures his worth in throughput. The 6:05 train to Manchester Piccadilly, the laptop open before Stockport. The board pack. The audit. The endless reconciliation of a life run as a going concern. Weekends he tells himself are for the family but his mind is doing the management accounts in the background, and his wife and children have learned to read the difference between Alan-present and Alan-here-but-running-the-numbers, which is most of the time.
He met Diane at a New Year's party in 1990; she was sparky and impatient and saw straight through his early bluster, and he loved her for that, genuinely, fiercely, once. They have a big house near Wilmslow that cost a great deal and that Alan experiences mostly as a set of standing orders. Three children: the two eldest gone, one in finance (of course), one in marketing in London; the youngest, Sophie, sixteen, the unexpected late one, still at home. It is Sophie who has started to register her father's absence most sharply, because she is the one still at the dinner table watching him not be there.
The detail that carries Alan: at dinner he eats fast, mechanically, and his eyes do a particular thing — they drop and slide, again and again, to the middle distance just past Diane's shoulder, where the numbers are. Sophie clocked it months ago and now narrates it cruelly and accurately. Dad's gone to the spreadsheet again. She'll say something — about a friend, a band, a thing that happened — and watch the mm land late, and she's started simply not telling him things, which Alan has not noticed, which is the whole problem in miniature. He tasted nothing of the meal Diane cooked for his birthday last month. He said it was lovely. He could not have told you what it was.
He had, once, a thing he was fully in — Saturday mornings as a boy, the football results coming in on the radio, his father marking the pools coupon, the held-breath attention of it. And later, his own playing: Sunday league into his thirties, the one arena where Alan was wholly present, wholly in his body and the moment, no numbers, just the next pass. He stopped at thirty-six — knee, work, the usual erosion. He still has the boots. Not as a relic he visits; he's not sentimental. They're just in the loft, and once a year when he goes up for the Christmas decorations his torch catches them and something passes through him, quick and unnamed, before he carries the boxes down.
Last week Diane said, evenly, over the washing up, you weren't really at Tom's wedding, were you, and it wasn't a row, that was the awful thing, it was just an observation, delivered without much heat because the heat had gone out of expecting otherwise. And Alan stood with a wet plate and tried to remember the wedding and found mostly the spreadsheet of it — the cost, the logistics — and very little of his eldest son's face as he said his vows. He said of course I was. He dried the plate. The forecast resumed.
A man so busy keeping the books of his life that he has forgotten to be present at any of its actual transactions, and the boots in the loft are the last record of a time he was all the way here.

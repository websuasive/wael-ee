# Daniel — Avatar

Daniel turns 49 in August. He works from a glass-fronted office in a business park outside Milton Keynes two days a week and from the kitchen table in Hitchin the other three, and if you asked him how work was he'd say "same as ever, mate, keeping the wheels on," and he'd mean it more than he meant to. He is Head of Operations for a homewares chain — 73 stores, three distribution centres, roughly 1,400 staff at last count — and he has been in and around retail operations since he was 22. What he does, essentially, is make sure the right number of cushions arrive at the right shops on the right days without anyone having to think about it. The not-thinking-about-it is the point. If anyone at the top of the company ever thinks specifically about operations, something has already gone wrong.

He grew up in West Bridgford, just outside Nottingham — three-bed semi on a quiet road, father a regional manager for a builder's merchant, mother a primary school teacher who later went into admin when the chaos of classrooms stopped being worth it. Only child. His father, Colin, was the kind of man who washed his Vauxhall every Sunday and once, famously, returned a Christmas jumper to M&S because a single thread had come loose on the sleeve. Colin believed in doing things properly. He died of a heart attack at 71, in 2019, on the patio he'd laid himself two summers before. Daniel thinks about him more than he expected to.

Daniel was the quiet one at school. Not unpopular — he played rugby for a bit, badly, and he could hold his own in the sixth-form common room — but he was never the one talking. He watched. Teachers liked him in the way teachers like boys who get on with it. He did his A-levels, got decent grades without heroics, and went to Nottingham Trent to read Business Studies because it seemed like the sensible thing and because his father had been pleased. He remembers his father shaking his hand on results day like Daniel had signed a contract.

At Trent he drank more than he'd expected to, played five-a-side on Tuesdays, had two girlfriends over three years, and realised somewhere in his second year that the modules he actually liked were the ones about how things got from A to B. Supply chain. Operations management. The maths of how a business actually works when you stop telling yourself stories about brands and culture. He liked the fact that the answers were real. Either the lorry arrived or it didn't. Either the stock was on the shelf or it wasn't. You could tell, at the end of the day, whether you'd got it right.

He joined a graduate scheme at a now-defunct electricals chain in 1998. Lived in a shared house in Leicester. Spent six months on the shop floor at a branch in Coventry, which he hated at the time and now, strangely, remembers fondly — the banter, the boredom, the old lifer who taught him how to spot shoplifters by their shoes. He moved on after two years, went to a supermarket operations team in Milton Keynes, spent four years there, then another four at a high-street fashion chain that has since been absorbed twice and doesn't exist any more. By the time he was 32, he was running the distribution piece for a homewares group — smaller than the one he's at now — and Caroline was pregnant, and they were buying a house in Hitchin because she worked in St Albans and his office was in Luton, and Hitchin was the compromise that was slightly nicer than either of them would have chosen alone.

He met Caroline at a mutual friend's birthday in 2003. She was a project manager for a pharmaceuticals firm, two years older than him, already divorced from a first marriage that had lasted eighteen months and which she referred to, with her characteristic flatness, as "the mistake." Daniel liked her immediately and slightly feared her, which he has since come to think is probably how a good marriage starts. They were together for two years before they got engaged. Married in 2006 in a registry office and a pub, because neither of them wanted a fuss, and because Caroline said weddings were a tax on the stupid. Thomas was born in 2009.

The years Thomas was small are now a blur Daniel doesn't entirely trust. He remembers being tired. He remembers the extension they put on the back of the house in 2013, during which Caroline discovered she was pregnant again and then, at eleven weeks, wasn't. They had agreed, before, that they'd try for a second. After, they stopped discussing it. It became one of those things in a marriage that exists in the room without being looked at, like a piece of furniture that never quite found the right wall. Thomas is 16 now and an only child, and Daniel has not, if he's honest, thought about the lost pregnancy in about six years. It comes back to him every so often, usually when he sees a boy of seven or eight with a smaller sister trailing behind.

His career, through his thirties, was what people call building. He switched companies twice, levelled up each time, learned how to chair a meeting without apologising, learned how to say no to things, learned how to sack people, which he is still not good at and which he handles by being very practical about the paperwork and very briefly human about everything else. He got his current job in 2019, six months before the pandemic, and the pandemic turned out to be, professionally, the defining period of his career. Supply chains broke. Shipping went mad. Warehouses needed restructuring. He worked harder than he'd ever worked in his life, on three hours' sleep some weeks, and held it together. The CEO at the time wrote him a bonus letter he still has in a drawer. It wasn't the money that mattered. It was the line that said, "you are the reason this place is still standing."

He knows that was the peak. Not because things are bad now — they aren't, particularly — but because you don't get another crisis like that, and what's left is maintenance. The business has contracted since. They closed two DCs and seven shops in 2023. His team is leaner. His days are meetings about things he has already thought through. He is, on any given Thursday, the most experienced person in any room he's in, and this turns out to be a thing no one warns you about: the loneliness of it.

He earns £135,000 plus bonus, drives a Volvo XC60 he picked because Caroline's sister said she felt safe in hers, and owns his house in Hitchin with about eleven years of mortgage left. The house is too big for three. They bought it in 2011, stretched, and spent five years feeling the stretch, and now it's comfortably affordable and still too big. There's a study he uses for work calls and a garage he has not parked in since approximately 2016, and a lawn he mows on Saturdays in a slightly aggressive way that Caroline has learned not to comment on.

His body has slowed. He noticed it first in his early forties, the way a night out took two days to recover from instead of one, and he ignored it, and now at 48 he can't quite ignore it. His knees click when he stands up from the sofa. He has put on about a stone since his mid-thirties and carries it mostly on the front, and he knows — as every man of his cohort knows — that he ought to do something about it, and he has a gym membership he uses roughly once a fortnight, always with the grim determination of someone paying a small fine. He runs, occasionally, on Sunday mornings, and has a complicated relationship with a podcast about the Second World War that he listens to while he does. He plays golf twice a month with the same three men, two of whom he has known for fifteen years, and they drink three pints afterwards and talk mostly about football and work. None of them have ever said anything serious to each other. This is not, in Daniel's mind, a problem. He likes it.

He and Caroline are, by any reasonable measure, fine. They like each other. They divide the household sensibly. She does bathrooms and kitchens and the social calendar; he does bins, cars, the garden, and Thomas's university research. They have sex about once a fortnight, slightly less than they used to, slightly more than Daniel would bet most of his friends. Caroline has become, in the last two or three years, more herself — taken up pottery on Thursdays, joined a book group, started speaking with a new kind of edge about her job, which she is quietly considering leaving. Daniel admires this and hasn't quite asked himself why he isn't doing the same. He has told himself it's because he doesn't have an equivalent urge. That has, until recently, been true.

Thomas is 16, in the middle of GCSEs, thinking about sixth-form colleges, already taller than Daniel by an inch. He is a quieter boy than Daniel was, more obviously clever, closer to Caroline. Daniel loves him with a steadiness that he doesn't make a fuss about. He takes him to rugby on Saturdays and drives him to the station when he goes into London to see his mates, and once, six months ago, Thomas asked him in the car what he'd wanted to be when he was 16, and Daniel answered honestly — "I didn't know, mate, I think I just wanted to not do anything embarrassing" — and Thomas laughed, and Daniel felt for about thirty seconds as if he'd said something true, and then they listened to the radio.

What he doesn't tell Thomas, and wouldn't know how to, is that the job is not the thing he'd recommend. Not because it's bad. Because it's used up. He has done it. He knows its shape. He can see the next ten years of it without having to look — more meetings, a reorg or two, a new CEO probably, a quieter winding-down toward a retirement he doesn't know how he'll feel about. He is too young for this knowledge. He knows he is. He tells himself it's a good problem to have.

About eighteen months ago, something started happening that he has not yet found the language for.

It began, as far as he can locate it, on a train back from Manchester in October 2024. He'd been up there for a supplier meeting, and he was sitting in a quiet carriage with a lukewarm coffee, and a man across the aisle was reading a novel whose cover Daniel noticed without quite registering. And something occurred to him. Not about the novel. About something else entirely — a scene, or an image, or the beginning of a scene: a man in a hotel corridor standing outside the wrong room, not knocking, not leaving. He couldn't have said where it came from. He sat with it for about an hour, watching it develop on its own, and when he got home he wrote it down in the back of his work notebook because he didn't want to forget it. He thought, then, that he was being silly.

Three weeks later it happened again. A woman on a bus, described from inside her own head, ten lines of interior monologue about a decision she was about to make. He wrote that one down too, in the same notebook, on a different page. By Christmas he had bought a Moleskine — black, soft-cover, which he felt mildly ridiculous buying and which he carried in his work bag next to his laptop — and had started writing things in it on the train, in the car park before meetings, once at three in the morning when a sentence turned up in his head and wouldn't go back.

He has no idea what is happening. He is not, he is confident, having a midlife crisis. He doesn't want to buy a motorbike. He doesn't want to leave Caroline. He doesn't want to quit his job and open a bakery in Cornwall. Everything in his life is roughly where he'd put it if he were arranging a life. But something, underneath, has changed direction without asking him, and he can feel it moving, and he doesn't know what it is.

The Moleskine is about three-quarters full now. Most of the notes are fragments — a line, an image, a character's name he made up, a scrap of dialogue he overheard in Waitrose that he couldn't let go of. Some of them are longer. Last month, in a week when Caroline was at a conference in Manchester and Thomas was at a mate's, he sat down at the kitchen table on a Sunday afternoon and wrote three pages, properly, of something. He isn't sure what it is — a short story, maybe, or the beginning of something longer — but it has a shape. A man, early 50s, who takes his father's old dog to be put down and on the drive home stops at a pub he hasn't been to in thirty years. He doesn't know why that was the thing that came out. His father is five years dead; they never had a dog.

He read the pages back a week later, on a Wednesday evening when Caroline was at her book group. He expected to hate them. He had steeled himself to hate them — had decided, actually, that hating them would be a relief, because it would mean he could stop doing this, whatever this was. He didn't hate them. They weren't, as far as he could tell, embarrassing. The sentences held together. The man in the story felt like a person, at least to him. He closed the notebook and sat for about twenty minutes not doing anything, and when Caroline came home he was watching football with the sound down and he said the evening had been fine.

He hasn't told her. He can't, yet, work out how to. The problem isn't that Caroline would be unkind — she wouldn't. The problem is that he doesn't know how to introduce a version of himself he's only just meeting. If he says "I've been writing," she'll ask what about, and he'll have to produce something, and the thing he'd have to produce is still moving, still unformed, and he's protective of it in a way that embarrasses him. He is 48 years old. He runs a £400 million operation. He should not be shy about a notebook. And yet.

There are evenings now when Caroline goes to bed at half ten and he says he'll be up in a minute, and he sits in the kitchen with the lamp on and the dishwasher humming, and he takes the Moleskine out of his bag. Sometimes he writes. Sometimes he just reads over what's already there. He has started to notice things during the day he didn't used to notice — the way a woman at the checkout spoke to her teenage son, the particular blue of the sky at the end of January, a thing his colleague Raj said in a meeting that wasn't meant to be funny but was. He finds himself interested in people in a way he wasn't before, or hadn't let himself be. He watches Caroline sometimes and thinks about what she's actually like, underneath the life they share, and feels something that is somewhere between love and guilt, because he realises he hasn't thought about her in that way — as a mystery, rather than a partner — in about twelve years.

He hasn't told his three golf friends. He hasn't told his old schoolfriend Ben, who he sees twice a year for a curry in Nottingham and who is the closest thing he has to the friend he could tell things to. Ben is a GP and has his own wounds, and the two of them talk the way men of their generation talk, which is to say around things, with jokes, with just enough seriousness to acknowledge that something has been registered without either of them having to do anything about it. If Daniel told Ben about the Moleskine, Ben would take him seriously. Daniel knows this and hasn't told him.

He thinks sometimes about his father. Colin would not have understood. Colin read the paper and the occasional Wilbur Smith and considered writing to be a thing other people did for money, and Daniel is glad, in a complicated way, that his father isn't alive to know about this, and is also sad that he can't ask him. He thinks about his mother, who is 79 now, living in a flat in West Bridgford, sharper than she lets on, and he thinks she'd be pleased — pleased in the way she was pleased when he came home from primary school with a painting — and he isn't sure he could stand that, either.

What he's starting to let himself think — not often, not clearly, but occasionally, in the kitchen at eleven o'clock on a Wednesday — is that this might be something. Not a career. Not a plan. Just something. A thing he does that is his, that no one else at work or at home has any particular claim over, that he didn't know was in him. He doesn't let himself think about where it goes. He doesn't let himself imagine finishing something, or showing it to anyone, or — God forbid — publishing it. The thought of any of that makes him feel like a fool. But the writing itself, the sitting-down and the trying, has started to feel like the most alive thing in his week, and he has noticed this, and he has not yet told anyone, including himself, not quite.

It's a Tuesday in April. He has a meeting in the morning about the summer stock plan, which he can do with about sixty percent of his brain. Caroline is at work. Thomas is at school. He drives to the office listening to the radio, and somewhere on the M1 just south of Milton Keynes a line arrives in his head, complete, the kind of line he has no use for at work and would once have forgotten by the time he got there. He reaches for the notebook on the passenger seat and writes it down in the car park when he arrives. Then he walks into the building. He is, for about another ten minutes, lighter than he has any reason to be, and doesn't remark on it to himself, and doesn't need to.

Summary: A steady, self-effacing English operations man who has spent his life making other people's plans work, quietly discovering at 48 that something of his own has started to show up without permission — and trying, with some embarrassment, to let it.

Martin Pheasey — Engineering Manager, 55
Martin is from Coventry, from a family that made things — his grandfather at the car plant, his father a toolmaker with hands like baseball mitts and a fierce, inarticulate pride in precision. Martin was the one who went further: a polytechnic degree in mechanical engineering, a graduate scheme, a steady climb into management at a tier-one automotive supplier near Warwick. He runs a department now. He is, by universal agreement, the sensible one — the man you put in the room when things are on fire because Martin will not catch fire. He will look at the problem, take the heat out of it, and find the reasonable path. It is a real gift and it has eaten him alive so slowly that neither he nor anyone else has noticed the meal.
The narrowing in Martin is the narrowing of a man who edits himself before the thought has fully formed. Somewhere — he could not date it — he installed a small internal official whose job is to vet every impulse for foolishness, and the official has been promoted over the years until it now intercepts wants at the source. Someone at work floats an idea and Martin can feel a flicker of enthusiasm rise, and almost simultaneously feel it checked, smoothed, brought down to a measured that could work, let's not get ahead of ourselves. He is the one who says let's not get ahead of ourselves. He has said it so many times that he no longer has any idea what was ahead, or whether he wanted to get there.
He doesn't risk looking keen. Keenness is for younger men, for the graduates with their phones held up at the test track filming things. Martin has a dignity reflex so finely tuned he experiences it as personality. He won't be the one to suggest the pub, won't be the first to laugh too loud, won't put himself forward for the thing he might actually love in case he is no good at it in front of people. Safer to be reliable. Reliable cannot be embarrassed.
He met Sue at a friend's twenty-first; she was — is — funnier than him, and married him partly, she once joked, because he was the only boy in the room not trying to be funny. They've a 1970s detached in a Warwick village, two kids: Chloe, twenty-three, just moved out to Birmingham, and Ben, nineteen, at Sheffield. The house has gone quieter with Ben's leaving and Martin has noticed that the quiet doesn't bother him the way he'd expected, and has not let himself wonder why. The marriage is decent, low-conflict, slightly flat. Sue has stopped suggesting things — weekends away, having people round — because the suggesting tired her out against Martin's gentle, reasonable resistance. We could. Maybe in the spring. Spring comes and goes.
In the garage, behind the lawnmower and the cardboard boxes that came from his dad's house when he died — no, his dad is alive, eighty-eight in a home, the boxes are from the clear-out of the family house — is Martin's motorbike. A 1981 Triumph Bonneville, partly restored. He bought it at thirty-eight in a flush of something. He got the engine running, got it gleaming, took it out twice, and then a colleague made a mild joke about midlife and Martin laughed along and put it back in the garage and there it sits under a sheet. He goes in there for the lawnmower. He does not look at the shape under the sheet, the way you don't look directly at a thing you've decided not to want. The MOT lapsed in 2009.
He plays things safe with his own pleasures so consistently that pleasure itself has become slightly suspect. On holiday in Pembrokeshire last year Sue went coasteering with Ben — leaping off rocks into the sea, shrieking — and Martin watched from the path holding the bags, and felt a want rise so sharp it surprised him, and the official stepped in: you'd look ridiculous, you'd hurt your back, someone has to hold the bags. He held the bags. On the drive home he was quiet, and Sue asked if he was alright, and he said just tired, which is the lid he keeps on the jar.
A man who has been so reasonable for so long that his own desires now arrive pre-apologised-for, and the bike under the sheet rusts in time with him.

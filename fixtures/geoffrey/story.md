Geoffrey Hartwell — Solicitor, 56
Geoffrey grew up in Shrewsbury, the elder son of a bank manager who wore the same three suits in rotation and believed that a man's worth was measured by whether other people could rely on him. The lesson took. By eleven Geoffrey was the boy who remembered the dentist appointments, who reminded his mother to defrost the chicken, who could be trusted with the spare key. He went to the grammar school, then to Nottingham to read law, and came back to the borders because the south never quite fit — too quick, too loud, too much elbowing. He liked the idea of being a known quantity in a place that knew him.
He is a partner now at a firm on the high street of a market town in Shropshire, the kind with a coaching inn and a Tuesday cattle market and a Boots that used to be something else. His name is on the brass plate by the door, slightly green at the screw-holes. He does wills, probate, the conveyancing of farms that have been in families for two hundred years, the occasional acrimonious boundary dispute between neighbours who will still have to wave at each other across the lane afterward. He is good at it. He is good at it in the specific way of a man who has made himself indispensable: he holds the histories, he knows which widow's son is not to be trusted with the proceeds, he keeps the threads.
The trouble — though Geoffrey would not call it trouble, would barely call it anything — is that he holds everyone's threads and has somehow misplaced his own. His diary, a real one, leather, refilled each January, is a dense weave of other people's needs. The clients, yes, but also the parish council he chairs, the school governors he sits with though his own children left a decade ago, the cricket club accounts, his mother's care home and its small administrations, his wife's elderly aunt in Telford. He says yes the way other men breathe. Someone asks and the yes is out of him before he has consulted any inner register of want, because he is not sure the register is still working. When a rare empty Saturday appears he feels not relief but a faint unease, like a phone with no signal.
He met Marion at a friend's wedding in 1991; she was reading something on the lawn while everyone else danced, and he liked that she didn't look up straightaway. They married, bought the Edwardian semi on the good side of town, raised two children — Tom, now in Bristol doing something in logistics, and Hannah, a teacher in Leeds with a baby Geoffrey has held four times. The marriage is intact. The marriage is, he would say, fine. But somewhere in the last fifteen years it became an institution he co-manages rather than a thing he is inside of. He and Marion are superb at logistics. They divide the world cleanly — she does the house and the social diary, he does money and cars and the elderly relatives — and they meet at the borders to exchange information. Did you ring the roofer. The Pearsons can't do the 14th. Your mother's chiropodist moved. They are colleagues running a small successful firm called their life. He cannot remember the last time he asked her a question to which he didn't already know, or need, the answer.
In the evenings he comes home at 6:40, hangs his coat on the third peg, and pours one glass of red — never two on a weeknight, a rule he set himself years ago and has never examined. They eat at the kitchen island. He asks how her day was and she tells him and he is already thinking about the Fenwick completion. There is a downstairs room they call the study, and in it, on the shelf above the desk, is a box of his father's fly-fishing reels — Hardy, brass, beautiful, oiled once by Geoffrey in 2009 and not since. His father took him to the Teme on summer evenings and stood behind him guiding his wrist, and those evenings are among the few memories Geoffrey can summon that have heat in them. He keeps meaning to get back to the river. He has been keeping meaning to for eleven years. The reels sit in their box and he dusts the box.
What he would notice, if he sat still long enough to notice anything — and stillness is the one appointment he never keeps — is that he is present for no one, including himself. Marion has started spending Thursday evenings at a choir, and he registered this as a logistical fact (he eats earlier those nights) rather than as information about a woman he is married to. Hannah told him on the phone last month that she'd been finding the baby hard, and he heard himself say well, you'll get there in the same voice he uses for nervous clients, and felt, briefly, the door of something close. He filed it. He files everything. There is so much to hold.
He has spent thirty years being the man everyone can lean on, and has quietly become a wall — load-bearing, weatherworn, and entirely unvisited.

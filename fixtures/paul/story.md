Paul Sankey — Senior HR Director, 53
Paul is from Liverpool, working-class made good, the first in his family to do an office job that didn't involve a hard hat. He was the warm one, the peacemaker, the lad who could read a room and smooth a falling-out — gifts that came from being the middle of five and learning early to manage the weather of a crowded house. Those gifts became a career. He went into personnel when it was still called that, and rose, and is now HR director of a large logistics firm headquartered outside Manchester, a man whose whole professional life is the skilled, genuine attending to other people's needs.
He is good at it the way water is good at finding the level. In a redundancy round he is the one who breaks it gently and means the gentleness. He remembers everyone's children's names. He notices when someone's gone quiet and asks the right question in the right doorway. He is, in the building, beloved — and the thing nobody sees, the thing Paul himself only half-sees, is that the attending has eaten the man it came from. He has collapsed so completely into the role of the one-who-tends that the off-duty Paul, the daft, enthusiastic, opinionated Paul, has gone quiet for want of being asked out.
Because that is the trap of being for everyone: nobody is for you. Paul spends his days being curious about others — and the curiosity is real, that's what makes it work — and arrives home each evening with the muscle already spent. So at home he is the same, only emptier: he asks his wife about her day, he asks the kids about theirs, he organises and fixes and smooths, and he could not tell you the last time anyone asked him a question that went past good day? and waited for a real answer. He's not sure he'd know how to give one. The inner Paul has been on mute so long he's gone a bit muffled even to himself.
He met Karen at twenty-three, Liverpool, a club night, and she'll still tell the story of how he made her laugh in the queue. They have three: a son, twenty-five, in Manchester; a daughter, twenty-two, just graduated; and a late surprise, Erin, fourteen, who is changing weekly into a person Paul realises, with a small shock, he is not actually keeping up with. He provides for Erin beautifully. He does the lifts, the money, the kit. But she said something at tea last week — a strong, strange opinion about something online — and Paul fixed his face into the warm-listening face he wears at work and said that's interesting, love, and realised even as he said it that he had stopped being genuinely curious about his own daughter, had started managing her like a stakeholder. The realisation went in like a splinter and he's been turning it over since.
The marriage to Karen is affectionate and tired and run on logistics — same as the others, though Paul, being who he is, knows it more clearly. They are warm with each other but they are for the household more than they are with each other; the last time they talked about anything that wasn't a child or a bill, neither could say. He is the man under the role and the role has nearly set, like resin.
The specific that carries Paul: he does an impression. Years ago he was funny — properly, get-up-at-the-do funny, did voices, did his Uncle Frank, had the family in bits. There's a wedding video from 2003 where Paul has the whole room. Erin found it last year and watched it baffled — Dad, you were funny — the past tense landing on Paul like a soft punch. He doesn't do the voices anymore. He's not sure when he stopped. Somewhere in becoming the steady one, the funny one was quietly retired, and now when he feels a quip rise he checks it, not from reasonableness like the engineer, but because it doesn't fit the shape he's set into, the shape everyone leans on.
What makes Paul the most poignant of the five is that he half-knows. He sat in a leadership offsite last month doing an exercise where they were asked what lights you up and Paul, who could fill in any colleague's answer instantly, sat with his pen over the blank and felt the cold draught of not knowing. He wrote family because it was the right answer. He stared at it. He knew it was the right answer and not a true one, and the gap between those two things is the size of the man he's mislaid.
A man who became so fluent in everyone else's needs that his own went quiet, and who is just now, faintly, beginning to hear the silence.

# Mark — Avatar

The alarm goes at quarter to five and he's up before it finishes, because Julia's asleep next to him and he's learned to kill it in one movement. Downstairs in the kitchen he makes coffee with the Aeropress he ordered in 2017 and has used every morning since. He takes it into the study — which is really the narrow room off the hall, the one they never turned into a proper spare bedroom because they never had people to stay — and opens the Scrivener file, and from five until a little before seven he writes. This has been his life for nine years. Seven mornings a week, give or take, with Christmas Day the only dependable exception and even then sometimes not. He is fifty-one years old. He is a partner at a City firm specialising in commercial disputes, which is the thing he does for money; and he is a novelist, which is the thing he does for reasons he cannot fully explain and has stopped trying to.

At seven he showers. By eight he's on the Northern Line out of Highgate. By nine he's a different man altogether, and that's how he likes it, or at least how he's told himself he likes it for long enough that the two things don't fight.

He grew up in Sevenoaks, only son of a quantity surveyor and a sixth-form English teacher. His sister Helen was born when he was four; she went into nursing and now lives outside Bristol with two boys and a husband who farms rare-breed pigs, and Mark sees her twice a year at most and feels, each time, the small surprise of liking her more than he'd remembered. Their father was a decent, contained man who built a modest consultancy doing cost work for local authorities; the business ran on his reputation, and when he tried to hand it to a younger partner in 2011 the younger partner botched it inside two years and their father, by then sixty-eight, quietly wound the firm up and retired with less than he'd expected. He died in 2019 of a stroke, having spent his last six years reading military history and walking the dog and watching Newsnight. Mark still doesn't know what his father's inner life was. He suspects there wasn't much of one, or rather that if there was, his father had laid it down somewhere so thoroughly that no one, including himself, could find it again. Mark thinks about this more than he'd like.

He read English at Durham. It was the only thing he ever wanted to do. He wrote a dissertation on John Williams — the American novelist, not the composer — and for a long time afterwards thought of himself as someone who was going to write novels. He met Julia in his final year; she was reading history, a year below him, and already at twenty more purposeful than he was at twenty-two. They moved in together in 1998 in a damp flat in Stoke Newington and lived on very little, Julia working her way up from an editorial assistant job she'd talked herself into, Mark doing stringer work for a couple of trade magazines and writing the opening chapters of a novel he wouldn't finish. He had the manuscript in a drawer for about eighteen months. He gave up on it the weekend after his twenty-fifth birthday and didn't write another sentence of fiction for fifteen years. He thinks, now, that he was embarrassed. The novel was bad. It was bad in the specific way that a clever young man's first novel is bad — sentences that wanted to be admired, a narrator indistinguishable from himself, nothing actually at stake. He threw the drafts out when they moved into the Highgate house in 2009 and has never regretted it.

The law was a practical decision that felt, at twenty-five, like a defeat dressed up as adulthood. He did the GDL, then the LPC, did a training contract at a mid-sized firm he lateraled out of after qualifying, and found his way, through a series of moves he doesn't think of as choices so much as corridors, to the firm he's been at since he was thirty-one. He made partner at thirty-nine. Commercial disputes. High-value, often international, the kind of work where a case can run for four years and a single hearing involves three silks and a judge who has read less of the bundle than you'd like. He is good at it. He is competent in the particular way a certain sort of man is competent at this kind of work — excellent on paper, calm under pressure, able to hold seventeen moving parts in his head at once, not so thrusting that clients find him alarming. What he doesn't tell people, because there's no one to tell, is that the work has not interested him for about a decade. He can do it in a state he thinks of, privately, as neither on nor off — a car in neutral rolling down a gentle hill. He gets to the bottom, another case resolved, another invoice sent, and can't remember the last time his mind actually worked on something it wanted to work on. The partnership money is very good. That is the sentence he tends to land on when he thinks about any of this.

Julia is a publisher — she runs an imprint at one of the big houses, does well, has grown into her own career in the slow confident way Mark has always admired. They married in 2002, bought the Highgate house in 2009 on a mortgage that felt terrifying at the time and now feels, thanks to fifteen years of salary creep, like a round they won without quite meaning to. Their daughter Cara is sixteen and in her GCSE year; their son Ben is thirteen and uncomplicated in a way Mark is quietly grateful for. The marriage is in the state Mark thinks of, on his clearer days, as honest — meaning they tell each other the real things, when the real things come up, though the real things come up less often than they used to. On his less clear days he thinks they are simply running the house together, two competent managers of a small concern, and that the tenderness he remembers is archived rather than present. Julia is the only person who reads his drafts. She reads them carefully and tells him what is wrong with them and is almost always right. This is the thing he would find hardest to explain to anyone who thought their marriage was thin.

Nine years ago — he was forty-two, Cara was seven, Ben was four — he started writing again. There was no single reason, though he has tried to assemble one for Julia's benefit and his own, and the reasons he offers depend on his mood. The one he believes most is small and slightly humiliating. A friend from Durham — Dan, a man he had always found slightly ridiculous — had published a novel that year with a respectable mid-sized house, and Mark had read it in a single long evening on a sofa in Cornwall while Julia was asleep upstairs, and at the end of the novel, which was fine, better than fine, he had sat with the book closed on his lap and felt a clean specific envy of a kind he hadn't felt in many years. It wasn't that the book was brilliant. It was that Dan had done it. Dan, who had less than Mark in every way Mark had been quietly counting, had done the one thing Mark had given up. He went home from that holiday and on the following Monday morning he got up at five and wrote for ninety minutes before work, and nine years later he is still doing it.

The first novel took four years. It was about two brothers clearing out their father's house in Norfolk after the father's death — Mark's own father was still alive at the time, a fact he thinks about — and it was quiet, slow, carefully done. He queried agents in the autumn of 2018 under the name Peter Shawcross, a name he assembled from his grandfather on his mother's side and a street in Sevenoaks. He signed with a thoughtful woman at a small agency. The book went out and was turned down everywhere and eventually landed at a small literary press in Norwich who published it in 2019. They printed 750 copies. They sold, he was told later, around four hundred. He has a box of twenty under his desk in the study and has never told anyone at the firm about it. Only Julia, and Dan, and the agent, and Helen, know. He has not told his mother, who would be proud in a way he doesn't want to manage. The book got two reviews, both short, both decent. He read them in a toilet cubicle at work on the day they came out and then tried not to think about them for about six months.

The second novel took four years also, finishing last year. It's with the agent now. It's larger, structurally more ambitious — something about a long commercial dispute and a young lawyer who realises, around year three, that she is working for the worse party. It is the best thing he has done. He knows this with the certainty he has learned, since starting again, to trust. The agent is submitting it gradually and carefully. He is trying not to think about it. He is starting a third.

This is the thing that complicates everything else.

Because if the writing were a hobby he could manage it the way other partners manage theirs — the cycling, the wine cellar, the golf, the one who flies gliders at weekends, the one who paints very badly in Mallorca every August. Mark could put it in its box and be grateful. But it isn't a hobby. The writing is the thing that makes the rest of his life tolerable, and he has known this for at least four years and now knows it so plainly that it has become a kind of pressure. The mornings are not enough. He used to be able to make the mornings enough by telling himself that two hours a day over a decade adds up to something, and it does, and he has made two novels out of it, but the person who did that was forty-two and forty-six and fifty. The person doing it at fifty-one has started to feel the arithmetic differently. He has fifteen working years left if he wants the pension, twenty if he has to, and he does not have forty good mornings left in him. He has, at the outside, some smaller number he prefers not to specify.

What he wants, and he has started to be able to say this to himself, is three things, all of them real, none of them bigger than the others. He wants to write more seriously. He wants his time to be his. He wants to live. These sound, when he lists them, like variations of a single thing, but they are not. The writing is the writing — more hours, more attention, the ability to carry a book in his head through the day instead of having to reboot it every twenty-four hours. The time question is different, and older; he is profoundly tired, in a way that has grown rather than faded, of his days being shaped by other people's demands — the client who needs a call at six on a Thursday, the meeting he didn't call, the weekend obliterated because a regulator has moved a deadline. And the living question is the one that hurts most to look at, because he has not been properly anywhere in eight years. He has not taken a full week off without his phone in longer than that. His friend Rob — who took a sabbatical in 2021 and never fully came back — has a cottage outside Penzance, works three days a week for a charity, and looks, when Mark sees him, the way Mark looked in his early thirties. Mark does not let himself hate Rob. He doesn't quite manage to not envy him.

He cannot unpick any of this without unpicking all of it. This is what he has started to see, though he has not yet said it out loud to Julia. The firm is not a place you work three days a week. Partners cannot sabbatical; partners bill. The money they live on is partner money; the Highgate mortgage is a thing they completed with partner money in mind. Cara will go to university in two years and Ben four years after that, and the economics of a reduced Mark are not the economics of their household. He has run the numbers several times, in the study, at five in the morning, with the novel file open behind the calculator and nothing written that morning at all. The numbers almost work and don't quite. They would work if Julia earned more, or if they moved, or if he took some other kind of legal work at something less than partner money — all of which are real options, and all of which are the kind of decision he has, historically, not been able to make.

He doesn't talk about any of this at work. When a colleague asks how he is, he says "Living the dream, mate," and means about forty per cent of it. There is a partner his age — Richard — who says he is going to retire at fifty-five, and nobody believes him, and Richard knows nobody believes him. Mark thinks about Richard more than he'd like. He also thinks about the managing partner, who is sixty-four and whose wife died last year and who has not taken a day off in four months.

On Tuesday evenings, which is a slightly random fact but true, he gets home around eight, eats whatever Julia and the kids have made or left, sits with Ben for a bit while Ben plays something on the PS5 and tries to explain what's happening on screen, says goodnight to Cara through her door because she is sixteen and closed, and then at ten, when Julia goes up, he stays downstairs. He has a glass of wine. Not two. He is careful about two. He reads. He reads literary novels in small careful sips, the way a man in recovery might look at a menu. He doesn't watch much television. Sometimes he opens the novel file again, though rarely; he has learned the evening isn't where the work happens. Mostly he just sits with the book and the glass and the house quiet around him, and this is — though he would not say this to anyone — the part of the day he likes most, and also the part of the day that frightens him most, because he can feel himself, in the silence, wanting more of it. Wanting, actually, most of his life to look like this. Which is not a thing a man with a mortgage in Highgate and two teenagers and a demanding firm is supposed to want.

His body has started to speak to him in ways it didn't. His back, chiefly — a particular ache that arrives at the end of a long day in the office chair and has started to carry into the morning. He has been meaning to do something about it for two years. He has not. He used to run; he has not run, properly, in about three years. His GP asked him at his last check whether he was managing stress and he said he was, which was neither a lie nor the truth but the thing you say. His cholesterol is borderline. He drinks somewhat more than he should but not catastrophically more. He is not in bad shape for fifty-one. He is not in the shape he was at forty-one, and he can feel the gradient.

His closest friends are Rob, who moved to Cornwall; Dan, who published the novel that started everything and has published two more since; and Will, who he lived with at Durham and who has been a GP in Leeds for twenty years and is the one who, if Mark were to ring and say I am not alright, would understand it without requiring Mark to explain. Mark has not rung Will in about ten months. He does not tend to ring Will when he is not alright. He rings Will when he is having a good week and wants to chat.

He is close to Julia in the honest way of long marriages, and not at all close to her in the way he once was. He does not tell her the fullness of what he is thinking because some of it is still forming and some of it is, he thinks, too frightening to speak aloud to a woman who has built a life on him being the person he has been. He suspects she already knows. He suspects she is waiting for him to say it. He suspects she is also, possibly, slightly dreading it. This is the kind of thing only a long-married couple can suspect about each other simultaneously and silently.

His mother is seventy-eight and living alone in Sevenoaks and sharp as she ever was, and he does not visit as often as he should. He is going down next weekend. He has been meaning to go down for seven weeks.

What he is starting to let himself look at — and this is new, this year, possibly this month — is the possibility that the compression he lives inside is not a permanent condition. That the three things he wants are not three greedy demands but a single shape of a life he could, with enough nerve, actually arrange. Not immediately. Not without cost. But arrange. The novels would be more than a thing done before work. His time would contain long quiet stretches in which nothing was required of him. He would go to the Hebrides, or Greece, or a cottage in the Peak District for a full slow month, and read, and walk, and write, and sit with Julia somewhere that wasn't a kitchen in Highgate on a weeknight. He would not, he thinks, have to blow up what he has. He thinks — he is not sure, but he thinks — that the thing might be unpickable without rupture, if he can find the thread.

He has not pulled the thread. He is still sitting downstairs at ten past eleven on a Tuesday with an empty glass and a book he's not reading, listening to the fridge, and in six hours the alarm will go, and he will get up, and he will write, and then he will put on the suit and go in. And so on, until something gives, or doesn't.

Summary: A fifty-one-year-old City lawyer who has been quietly building a second life as a novelist for nine years, and has started to understand that the life he actually wants cannot be had in the margins of the one he's got.
